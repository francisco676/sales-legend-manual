the thesis

the single biggest unlock for selling to executives.

at any price point above $500/mo sold to an executive: executives don't buy software. they buy a confident expert who makes them feel understood. everything in this manual serves that.

most founders try to learn a better pitch. that's the wrong direction. the close rate leak isn't in your pitch. it's in your questions, your pauses, your tonality, and whether the buyer said the cost of the status quo out loud in their own words before you showed them a slide.

core principle

diagnose before you prescribe. reps who spend 30 to 40% of discovery asking real problem questions close at roughly 2× the rate of those who spend under 20%. before any screen share, the exec should have articulated the gap between their current reality and the outcome they want, in their own words.

tier 1 · non-negotiables

the eight rules every master agrees on.

universal across Voss, Rackham, Sandler, Orlob, Dixon, Hormozi, Klaff, Miner, Enns, Keenan. if you only drill eight things, drill these.

01
diagnose before you prescribe

every source agrees. SPIN, Voss, Sandler, Orlob, Dixon, Keenan, Miner, Hormozi. reps who spend 30 to 40% of the first call on real discovery close at roughly 2× the rate of those who rush to pitch.

02
implication questions beat situation questions

Rackham's most-cited finding: "what does this problem cost you?" correlates with closed-won at dramatically higher rates than "how many reps do you have?" in deals above $1K. top reps ask 3 to 4 per discovery call. bottom reps ask 1 or 2.

03
talk less than the buyer

Gong data: top performers on discovery talk 43% and listen 57%. bottom performers flip it. on demos, the top cap their monologues at 60 to 90 seconds before re-engaging.

04
next steps must be specific, dated, confirmed on the call

deals with a confirmed next step close 80%+ more often than deals with vague "i'll follow up." grab your calendar live. send the invite before the hangup.

05
tonality carries 70% of the message

Voss's late-night FM DJ voice establishes authority. Miner's curious and concerned tones unlock truth. deals where rep sentiment stays calm and steady close at higher rates than deals with anxious energy spikes. your voice is a tool. most founders never train it.

06
quantify the problem in the buyer's own words

they must say, out loud, the dollar, time, or reputational cost of the status quo. "if you keep posting once a month for 12 months, what does that cost you in pipeline, in inbound, in the eyes of your board?" if they can't quantify, the deal isn't real.

07
frame control: set the agenda and the rules

first 60 seconds: why we're here, how long, what happens at the end, permission to say no. Sandler's upfront contract: "at the end of today you'll either say 'let's start Monday' or 'not a fit.' either answer is perfect. fair?"

08
price the value, not the service

Hormozi's value equation: value = (dream outcome × likelihood) / (time delay × effort). make the gap between value and price so wide they feel dumb not buying. one enterprise deal closed by an exec with a 10K LinkedIn following pays for 5 years of ghostwriting.

tier 2 · force multipliers

the eight moves that compound the basics.

09
"no" is the start, not the end

chasing yes puts buyers on defense. inviting no relaxes them. "would it be crazy to think we could get your first post live this week?" deals with multiple early nos that convert later outperform all-yes deals (which die quietly).

10
label emotions before they get weaponized

Voss's cheat code. "it sounds like you've been burned by agencies before." "it seems like you're skeptical anything can sound like you." labels disarm objections before they're spoken. target 3+ per demo.

11
calibrated questions transfer ownership

"how am i supposed to do that?" "what about this is important to you?" "what's the biggest challenge you face?" they force the buyer to solve your problem for you.

12
assumptive language only

winning phrases: when we implement, once we roll out, your first post, what we'll do together. losing phrases: if, should, probably, hopefully, kind of, sort of. audit your transcripts for hedging words. replace with: will, when, is, does.

13
multithread or lose

deals with 2+ buyer-side contacts close 34% more. with 3+, 58% more. ask: "besides you, who else touches the content review?"

14
act like you're screening them

Klaff's prizing, Sandler's negative reverse, Enns's expert positioning. "i only take on execs who'll actually post weekly. if you're going to ghost the drafts, i'd rather save us both the time. are you actually going to post?" the moment they're selling you on being a fit, you've won.

15
mutual action plans for anything past one call

shared doc, co-authored. "by nov 1 you send us 3 sample posts. by nov 3 we deliver 5 drafts. by nov 5 you post. go/no-go by nov 10." deals with MAPs close at roughly 2× the rate.

16
chase "that's right," not "you're right"

"you're right" means they want you to shut up. "that's right" means they feel genuinely understood. summarize their situation so well they say it. when they do, the deal is usually won.

tier 3 · specialist tactics

the six moves that separate 60% from 80% closers.

17
name a respected peer in the XY benchmark

Challenger's commercial teaching. "[named CMO at recognizable company] was where you are 6 months ago. 800 followers, no inbound. now getting 2 inbound demos per week from LinkedIn." specific, named, recent.

18
quantify the cost of delay, not just the problem

Dixon's JOLT research: indecision kills ~56% of B2B deals, not status quo. quantify delay: "every month you wait is a month your competitor's VP is in front of your next 10 buyers."

19
lean harder into fast delivery

if you deliver in 48 hours and agencies take 2 weeks, that's your proof and your pressure. "within 48 hours you'll see 5 drafts in your voice. if they don't sound like you, you don't pay."

20
permission-based micro-closes every 2 to 3 minutes

"sound reasonable?" "is that the kind of outcome you're after?" "should we keep going or is this not clicking?" you're training them to say yes and getting constant signal.

21
silence after pricing: at least 7 seconds

first to speak after the price loses. most founders fill the silence because it's uncomfortable. drill this. count to seven in your head. wait.

22
objection → label → calibrated question

when you hear "let me think about it" or "it's too expensive": never argue. "it sounds like you're not 100% there yet. what's the piece giving you pause?" universal across Voss, Miner, Braun, Sandler.

the gap

ten dimensions gong doesn't track.

conversation intelligence tools count questions, measure talk-time, and flag monologues. they miss the dimensions the masters actually coach on. this is what the Claude skill scores.

tonality

curious vs concerned vs apologetic. the FM DJ voice at price reveal. energy variance. most founders never audit how they sound.

question quality

situation vs implication (SPIN). labeled vs direct (Voss). calibrated vs leading. 20 closed questions beats nothing but not much.

pause architecture

pauses after questions force thinking. pauses after pricing force acceptance. pauses after objections stop rescue behavior. measurable from timestamps.

pain quantification

binary flag: did the buyer state the cost of the problem in their own words, in dollars, time, or reputation? if no, the deal is a coin flip.

next-step strength

not "did you set one." was it dated? calendar invite sent live? both sides' deliverables specific? decision criterion named?

frame ownership

agenda set in first 90 seconds? upfront contract used? time transitions controlled by you ("we have 10 minutes left")? or did the buyer drive?

multithreading signals

was anyone else mentioned? did you ask "who else touches this?" is there a named second contact post-call?

hedging vs assumptive ratio

think, might, maybe, probably, hopefully counted against when we, once you, your first post. the ratio predicts close rate.

label count

how many Voss-style labels ("it sounds like...", "it seems like...") did you use? target: 3+ per call.

rapport moments

longest buyer monologue (engagement proxy). count of buyer laughter and "yeah, exactly" affirmations. the "that's right" moment, if it happened.
